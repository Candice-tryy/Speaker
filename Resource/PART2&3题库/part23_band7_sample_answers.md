I’d like to talk about an unusual meal I had about two years ago, during a trip to a remote village in northern Vietnam. It was a late autumn evening, and I was travelling with a close friend from university. We had been hiking all day and ended up staying with a local family who ran a small homestay. The meal itself was unlike anything I’d ever experienced. The family prepared a traditional dish called “thắng cố” – a kind of stew made from horse meat and organs, cooked with local herbs and spices. I remember sitting on low wooden stools around a smoky fire, eating from a communal clay pot. The texture was quite chewy, and the flavour was strong and earthy, with a hint of bitterness from the herbs. What made it truly unusual was the setting and the company. We ate with the host family and a few other travellers, all sharing one pot using chopsticks and our hands. There was no proper table, just a bamboo mat on the dirt floor, and the only light came from the fire and a single oil lamp. The whole experience felt raw and authentic, far removed from any restaurant meal I’d had before. Reflecting on it now, I think the meal was unusual not just because of the strange ingredients, but because it broke all my usual routines. It forced me to step out of my comfort zone and connect with people through food in a very direct, unpolished way. I felt a mix of surprise, curiosity, and genuine gratitude – it’s a memory I still treasure.

### Q5. Do you think having dinner at home is a good idea？

Honestly, I think it depends on the situation. Having dinner at home is generally a good idea because it’s more affordable and you have full control over what you eat—like choosing fresh ingredients and avoiding too much oil or salt. For example, I often cook simple stir-fries with vegetables and chicken, which feels healthier than takeaway. That said, eating out occasionally can be nice for special occasions or trying new cuisines, so I wouldn’t say home dinners are always better. It’s more about balance—home for routine, restaurants for variety.

### Q6. What are the advantages and disadvantages of eating in restaurants?

Well, I’d say the main advantage of eating in restaurants is convenience—you just sit down, order, and the food comes, so there’s no cooking or washing up. For example, after a long day at work, I often grab a quick meal at a local noodle bar, which saves me at least an hour. Another plus is the variety; restaurants let you try dishes you’d never cook at home, like Thai green curry or grilled octopus. On the downside, it can be quite expensive. A simple pasta dish in a mid-range restaurant might cost five times what you’d spend making it yourself. Also, you have less control over ingredients—some places use too much salt or oil, which isn’t ideal if you’re watching your health. So, while restaurants offer ease and new flavours, they’re not always budget-friendly or healthy.

### Q7. Do people in your country value food culture?

Yes, I think people in my country—especially in recent years—have come to value food culture quite deeply. For example, traditional dishes like dumplings during Chinese New Year or mooncakes during the Mid-Autumn Festival are still prepared at home, not just bought from shops. People take time to learn family recipes from grandparents, and there's a growing interest in regional cuisines, like Sichuan or Cantonese food. However, I'd say there's also a contrast: while older generations focus on authenticity and seasonality, many younger people are more drawn to trendy food experiences or fast dining. So the value exists, but it's expressed differently across age groups.

### Q8. Do people in your country socialize in restaurants? Why?

Yes, absolutely. In China, restaurants are one of the most common places for socialising, whether it's with family, friends, or colleagues. Going out for a meal is seen as a way to strengthen relationships, especially during festivals or celebrations. For example, during the Lunar New Year, many families book a big round table at a restaurant and share dishes like whole fish and dumplings, which symbolise prosperity. Also, business lunches are very popular—people often close deals over hotpot or Peking duck because the relaxed atmosphere makes conversation easier. So, it's not just about eating; it's about bonding and showing respect through hospitality.

### Q9. Why do people choose to eat out instead of ordering takeout?

People choose to eat out rather than order takeout mainly for the social experience and atmosphere. When you go to a restaurant, you can interact face-to-face with friends or family, which feels more personal than eating alone at home. For instance, celebrating a birthday at a lively Italian restaurant with candlelight and background music creates a memorable event that takeout simply can't replicate. However, takeout is often more convenient for busy weeknights when you're tired. So the choice really depends on whether someone prioritises connection and ambience or practicality and time.

### Q10. Do people eat fast food at home?

Yes, people do eat fast food at home quite often. For example, many families order pizza or fried chicken for dinner when they don’t feel like cooking. I’ve seen friends heat up frozen burgers or instant noodles in their own kitchens, which is basically fast food eaten at home. However, it’s usually more of a convenient choice than a regular routine. Some people might do it once a week, especially on busy weeknights, while others prefer home-cooked meals most of the time. So, while it’s common, it’s not the only way people eat at home—it really depends on their lifestyle and schedule.

### Q11. What fast food is there in your country?

In my country, fast food is everywhere. You've got the big international chains like McDonald's, KFC, and Pizza Hut, but we also have a lot of local fast food. For example, *bánh mì* stalls are super common—they sell Vietnamese baguettes filled with pork, pickled vegetables, and cilantro, and you can get one in under a minute. Then there's *phở* served quickly in casual shops, though it's a bit slower than a burger. I'd say the most popular fast food is probably fried chicken from places like KFC or local spots that do it with rice and a spicy dip. It's cheap and filling, so students and office workers grab it a lot.
